This dataset contains clinical and metabolic measurements from patients screened for gallstone disease. The data includes demographic information, anthropometric measurements, body composition analysis, metabolic markers, and liver function tests. Each observation represents one patient.

Pre-selection of Variables

Variables to DROP:

  • Height and Weight: Redundant information since BMI is already calculated
  • Total Body Water, ECW, ICW, ECF/TBW: Multiple body water measurements provide redundant information
  • Bone Mass, Muscle Mass, Lean Mass, Body Protein Content: Body composition details not directly related to gallstone formation
  • AST, ALT, ALP, Creatinine, GFR, CRP, Hemoglobin: Secondary clinical markers; focusing on primary metabolic risk factors
  • Comorbidity, CAD, Hypothyroidism, Hyperlipidemia: Multiple disease indicators; keeping only Diabetes as key metabolic disease
  • Vitamin D: Limited direct connection to gallstone pathophysiology
  • VFR, Obesity %: Redundant with Visceral Fat Area and BMI

Variables to KEEP:

  • Gallstone Status: Response variable
  • Age, Gender: Key demographic predictors
  • BMI: Primary obesity measure
  • Glucose, Diabetes: Glucose metabolism indicators
  • Total Cholesterol, LDL, HDL, Triglycerides: Complete lipid profile (cholesterol forms gallstones)
  • Visceral Fat Area: Direct measure of abdominal obesity
  • Hepatic Fat Accumulation: Liver fat related to metabolic syndrome

Rationale: We focus on variables directly related to metabolic syndrome and gallstone pathophysiology while removing redundant measurements.

Section 2: Scientific Question

Research Question

We want to examine the association between gallstone disease (response) and metabolic risk factors including BMI, lipid profile (total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, triglycerides), visceral fat area, glucose metabolism markers (glucose levels, diabetes status), hepatic fat accumulation, and demographic factors (age, gender).

Response Variable

The response variable is Gallstone Status, a binary variable where 0 indicates no gallstone and 1 indicates gallstone present.

Focus: Inference or Prediction

The primary focus of this project is inference. We want to understand which metabolic and clinical factors are significantly associated with gallstone disease and quantify these associations. This will help identify high-risk individuals and understand the underlying metabolic connections to gallstone formation. While the model could be used for prediction, our main goal is to interpret the relationships between predictors and the outcome.

Visualization Interpretation

Why this plot is relevant:

This visualization explores five variables at once by using position (age, BMI), color (triglycerides), shape (gallstone status), and facets (diabetes, hepatic fat). Faceting by diabetes and hepatic fat lets me see whether gallstone patterns differ across these metabolic conditions. This multi-variable view is useful because gallstone disease likely involves multiple risk factors rather than just one.

Results obtained:

The plot shows that gallstone patients (triangles) and non-gallstone patients (circles) overlap considerably in age and BMI across all six panels. Most patients have low-to-moderate triglyceride levels (blue/teal colors), with only a few showing high levels (yellow colors). The top row (No Diabetes) has far more patients than the bottom row (Diabetes), and the Moderate hepatic fat panel appears to have the most observations overall. Among diabetes patients, gallstone cases seem to cluster more in the Moderate hepatic fat panel at higher BMI values. Overall, no single variable clearly separates gallstone from non-gallstone patients, suggesting that multiple factors may be involved.

What I learned:

Three things stand out from this visualization. First, the overlap between gallstone and non-gallstone patients across age and BMI suggests that these factors alone won't predict gallstone disease well. Second, the Moderate hepatic fat panel contains the most data and shows a noticeable concentration of gallstone cases, especially among diabetes patients. Third, most patients have relatively low triglyceride levels regardless of gallstone status, so extremely high triglycerides don't seem necessary for gallstone formation in this dataset. These patterns suggest I'll need a multivariable model to understand how these factors work together.

Section 4: Method and Plan

Proposed Method:

I will use logistic regression to model the binary response variable (gallstone presence/absence) as a function of the metabolic and demographic predictors.

Why is this method appropriate?

Logistic regression is appropriate because our response variable is binary (gallstone present or absent), and we want to understand how each predictor is associated with the log-odds of gallstone disease while controlling for other variables.

Assumptions required:

  1. The log-odds of the outcome have a linear relationship with the continuous predictors
  2. Observations are independent (each patient is measured once)
  3. No perfect multicollinearity among predictors (though some correlation is acceptable)

Limitations and weaknesses:

  1. Logistic regression assumes linear log-odds relationships, which may not capture complex non-linear patterns
  2. The model is sensitive to outliers and influential observations
  3. It may miss important interactions between predictors unless explicitly specified
  4. With 11 predictors and 319 observations, we have adequate sample size (~29 observations per predictor), but adding many interaction terms could lead to overfitting

Section 5: Computational Code and Output

I will fit a logistic regression model with gallstone status as the response and all selected metabolic/demographic variables as predictors. I will visualize the results using a coefficient plot showing odds ratios with confidence intervals.

# REVISED: Data cleaning to fix convergence warnings and fit model

# 1. Fix: Collapse rare Hepatic Fat levels (3 & 4) into Level 2 
# This prevents the "fitted probabilities 0 or 1" warnings seen in the previous draft
gallstone_clean <- gallstone %>%
  mutate(
    hepatic_fat_fixed = case_when(
      as.numeric(as.character(hepatic_fat)) >= 2 ~ "2+", # Group Levels 2, 3, 4 together
      TRUE ~ as.character(hepatic_fat)
    ),
    hepatic_fat_fixed = as.factor(hepatic_fat_fixed)
  )

# 2. Fit logistic regression model using the stable "hepatic_fat_fixed" variable
gallstone_model <- glm(
  gallstone ~ age + gender + bmi + glucose + diabetes + 
              cholesterol + ldl + hdl + triglycerides + 
              visceral_fat + hepatic_fat_fixed,
  data = gallstone_clean,
  family = binomial(link = "logit")
)

# Display model summary

# REVISED: Create professional coefficient plot

# Set dimensions
options(repr.plot.width = 10, repr.plot.height = 7)

# Prepare data for plotting
plot_data <- model_results %>%
  filter(term != "(Intercept)") %>%
  mutate(
    term_clean = case_when(
      term == "age" ~ "Age",
      term == "gender1" ~ "Gender (Male)",
      term == "bmi" ~ "BMI",
      term == "glucose" ~ "Glucose",
      term == "diabetes1" ~ "Diabetes",
      term == "cholesterol" ~ "Total Cholesterol",
      term == "ldl" ~ "LDL",
      term == "hdl" ~ "HDL",
      term == "triglycerides" ~ "Triglycerides",
      term == "visceral_fat" ~ "Visceral Fat Area",
      term == "hepatic_fat_fixed1" ~ "Hepatic Fat: Level 1",
      term == "hepatic_fat_fixed2+" ~ "Hepatic Fat: Level 2+ (Mod/Severe)",
      TRUE ~ term
    ),
    significant = ifelse(p.value < 0.05, "Significant", "Not Significant"),
    or_label = paste0("OR=", round(estimate, 2))
  )

# Create coefficient plot
coefficient_plot <- ggplot(plot_data, aes(y = reorder(term_clean, estimate))) +
  geom_vline(xintercept = 1, linetype = "dashed", color = "gray50", linewidth = 0.8) +
  geom_errorbar(aes(xmin = conf.low, xmax = conf.high, color = significant),
                width = 0, linewidth = 1.2) +
  geom_point(aes(x = estimate, color = significant), size = 4) +
  geom_text(aes(x = estimate, label = or_label), 
            hjust = -0.3, size = 3.5, color = "gray20") +
  scale_color_manual(
    values = c("Significant" = "#E1AF00", "Not Significant" = "#3B9AB2"),
    name = "P-value < 0.05"
  ) +
  scale_x_log10(breaks = c(0.25, 0.5, 1, 2, 4)) +
  labs(
    title = "Logistic Regression: Predictors of Gallstone Disease",
    subtitle = "Odds Ratios with 95% Confidence Intervals",
    x = "Odds Ratio (log scale)",
    y = NULL
  ) +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 12) +
  theme(
    plot.title = element_text(face = "bold", size = 14),
    axis.text.y = element_text(size = 11),
    legend.position = "bottom",
    panel.grid.minor = element_blank()
  )

Interpretation:

The logistic regression identifies three statistically significant risk factors ($p < 0.05$): Male gender (OR=1.95), Diabetes (OR=2.47), and Visceral Fat Area (OR=1.11 per unit). Interestingly, Hepatic Fat Level 1 appears significantly protective compared to Level 0 ($p=0.003$), while Moderate/Severe Hepatic Fat (Level 2+) shows a strong positive association (OR=1.85) that approaches but does not quite reach statistical significance ($p=0.066$) in this adjusted model. The VIF analysis confirms high multicollinearity among lipid markers (Cholesterol VIF=9.96, LDL VIF=7.62), explaining why these individual lipid variables appear non-significant despite their biological relevance.
